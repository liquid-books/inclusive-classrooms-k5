# Understanding Inclusion: What It Means and Why It Matters

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-infographic-inclusion-overview.png
:label: fig-ch01-infographic
:alt: Comprehensive infographic showing the five pillars of inclusive education in K-5 classrooms
:width: 80%
:align: center

The five pillars of inclusive education: legal foundations, diverse learners, universal design, family partnership, and culturally responsive teaching all converge on a single goal — every child belongs.
:::

Imagine your first day in your own classroom. Twenty-two children walk through the door. One uses a wheelchair. Three are English Language Learners who speak Spanish at home. Two have been identified with ADHD. One child has an Individualized Education Program (IEP) for a learning disability in reading. One was recently released from foster care and carries trauma you cannot yet see. Several are reading two grade levels above their peers. The rest are navigating all the ordinary, beautiful complexity of being five, six, seven, or eight years old.

Every single one of them is your student.

Not the special education teacher's students. Not the ELL specialist's students. Not the "good" students and the "difficult" students. Yours.

This is what inclusion actually means — and it is not a policy, a room, or a paperwork system. It is a *philosophy of teaching* that holds every child capable of growth, every learner deserving of belonging, and every classroom richer for the diversity it contains. This chapter lays the foundation for everything that follows in this book. We begin by defining what inclusion really is (and what it is not), tracing the history that made it possible, and building the case — moral, legal, and deeply practical — for why you, as a future educator, must make it the core of who you are.

## What Inclusion Really Means

The word "inclusion" is used so frequently in education that it has nearly lost its meaning. It appears in school mission statements, accreditation documents, professional development agendas, and federal law. But ask ten teachers what inclusion means in practice and you will get ten different answers — ranging from "the special ed kids are in my room now" to "I treat all children the same" to "every child has a right to be here, supported, and challenged."

Only the last answer is correct. And understanding *why* requires us to unpack three concepts that are often confused: **exclusion**, **integration**, and **inclusion**.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-inclusion-vs-integration.png
:label: fig-ch01-inclusion-vs-integration
:alt: Infographic comparing exclusion, integration, and full inclusion models in education
:width: 80%
:align: center

The evolution from exclusion to integration to full inclusion represents a fundamental shift — not just in *where* students are educated, but in how schools understand who belongs.
:::

### Exclusion: The Default for Most of History

For most of American educational history, children with disabilities, children of color, and children who did not fit the assumed "default" student profile were simply excluded. Separate schools, separate rooms, or no school at all. The message was unambiguous: *you do not belong here.*

This was not ancient history. As recently as the 1970s, more than one million children with disabilities in the United States were entirely excluded from public schools. Parents of children with Down syndrome, cerebral palsy, autism, or intellectual disabilities were routinely told that their child was "uneducable" and advised to consider residential institutions. Entire categories of human beings were written off before they ever had a chance.

### Integration: Getting in the Door, But Not Really Inside

The next phase — integration, also called "mainstreaming" — was a genuine step forward, but it fell short of transformation. Integration meant that students with disabilities (and students from marginalized communities) were *admitted* to general education settings, but the system itself did not change to welcome them.

Picture it this way: integration is being allowed to eat at the restaurant but seated next to the kitchen, given no menu translation, handed a dish you cannot eat, and expected to be grateful for the invitation. You are *in* the building. But you do not *belong* to it.

In educational terms, integration often meant that a student with a disability would spend most of their day in a separate resource room and "join" the general education class for non-academic subjects like art, lunch, or physical education. The general education classroom was not designed for them. The curriculum was not adapted for them. The teacher was not trained to teach them. They were present. They were not included.

### Full Inclusion: The System Changes, Not the Child

Full inclusion is a completely different paradigm. It does not ask, *"Can this child fit into our existing system?"* It asks, *"How do we design our system so that every child can thrive?"*

The distinction seems subtle. It is revolutionary.

In a fully inclusive classroom, the teacher designs instruction from the beginning to be accessible to learners across a range of abilities, backgrounds, languages, and learning profiles. Supports are built into the environment, not bolted on as afterthoughts. Every child is a full member of the classroom community — academically, socially, and emotionally.

::::{important}
**Inclusion is not the same as identical treatment.** Treating all children "the same" ignores the reality that children arrive at school with vastly different experiences, needs, and starting points. Equity requires differentiation — giving each child what *they* need, not what the mythical "average" student needs.
::::

## The Difference Between Equality and Equity

Before we go further, we need to address one of the most common misconceptions in education: the idea that fairness means sameness.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-equity-vs-equality.png
:label: fig-ch01-equity-vs-equality
:alt: Infographic illustrating the difference between equality, equity, and justice in education
:width: 80%
:align: center

Equality gives everyone the same thing. Equity gives everyone what they need. Justice removes the barriers that created the need in the first place.
:::

Imagine three students standing at a fence, trying to watch a baseball game. The fence is five feet tall.

- **Student A** is five feet, five inches tall. She can see just fine.
- **Student B** is four feet, eight inches tall. He can barely see over the top.
- **Student C** is four feet, two inches tall and uses a wheelchair. She cannot see at all.

**Equality** means giving each student an identical 12-inch wooden box to stand on. Student A can see even better. Student B can now see. Student C is still stuck — the box does not help someone in a wheelchair, and the fence is still a barrier.

**Equity** means giving each student *what they specifically need* to see the game. Student A gets nothing extra — she's fine. Student B gets a small step stool. Student C gets a ramp that elevates her wheelchair to the height of the fence.

**Justice** is building a transparent fence so everyone can see, eliminating the need for compensatory supports in the first place.

This framework is not abstract philosophy. It has direct implications for how you structure your classroom, how you deliver instruction, how you assess learning, and how you advocate for your students. An inclusive educator thinks in terms of equity — not sameness — and they think structurally, always asking what *systems* need to change rather than what children need to fix about themselves.

## A Brief History of Inclusive Education in America

Understanding where inclusion came from gives it weight and urgency. Every right that today's students with disabilities enjoy was fought for — often by parents, disability advocates, and civil rights leaders who refused to accept the status quo.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-history-timeline.png
:label: fig-ch01-history-timeline
:alt: Timeline of major milestones in the history of inclusive education in America from 1954 to 2015
:width: 80%
:align: center

Six decades of legal milestones — each one expanding the right of every child to a free, appropriate, publicly funded education in the least restrictive environment.
:::

### Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

The foundation of inclusive education is the same as the foundation of the civil rights movement in schools: *Brown v. Board of Education*, the landmark Supreme Court case that declared racially segregated public schools unconstitutional.

The Court's reasoning is worth reading closely. Chief Justice Earl Warren wrote: *"In these days, it is doubtful that any child may reasonably be expected to succeed in life if he is denied the opportunity of an education. Such an opportunity, where the state has undertaken to provide it, is a right which must be made available to all on equal terms."*

Replace "racially segregated" with "segregated by disability" and the argument is identical — which is exactly why disability rights advocates used *Brown* as their legal foundation for the battles that followed.

### The Pennsylvania and D.C. Cases (1971–1972)

Two landmark class action lawsuits in the early 1970s established, for the first time, that children with intellectual disabilities had a constitutional right to a public education.

**PARC v. Commonwealth of Pennsylvania (1971)** was filed on behalf of children with intellectual disabilities who had been excluded from Pennsylvania public schools under a state law that permitted schools to turn away any child deemed "uneducable." The case settled with a consent decree requiring Pennsylvania to provide all children with intellectual disabilities a free public education — and to place them in regular classrooms whenever possible.

**Mills v. Board of Education of D.C. (1972)** went further, establishing that the District of Columbia could not exclude children with disabilities even if it claimed a lack of funds. The court's ruling was unequivocal: fiscal constraints do not justify denying children their constitutional right to education.

These two cases changed the conversation nationally and directly inspired the federal legislation that followed.

### The Education for All Handicapped Children Act (1975)

In 1975, President Gerald Ford signed Public Law 94-142, the **Education for All Handicapped Children Act** — one of the most consequential pieces of education legislation in American history.

For the first time, the federal government mandated that all children with disabilities were entitled to:
- A **free and appropriate public education (FAPE)**
- An **Individualized Education Program (IEP)** outlining specific goals and services
- Placement in the **least restrictive environment (LRE)** — meaning, as close to the general education classroom as possible
- **Due process rights** allowing families to challenge school decisions

The "least restrictive environment" clause was transformative. It was a direct repudiation of the segregation model — an acknowledgment that children with disabilities belonged, by default, in general education classrooms, and that removing them required justification, not the reverse.

### IDEA: From Participation to Full Inclusion (1990–2004)

In 1990, PL 94-142 was reauthorized and renamed the **Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)**, a renaming that was itself significant — emphasizing the humanity of individuals, not their diagnoses. IDEA was strengthened again in 1997 and reauthorized as the **Individuals with Disabilities Education Improvement Act (IDEIA)** in 2004, with increased emphasis on high expectations, measurable outcomes, and access to the general education curriculum.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), also signed in 1990, broadened civil rights protections for people with disabilities across all areas of public life — including schools — and established Section 504, which provides accommodations for students with disabilities who do not qualify for special education services under IDEA.

### Every Student Succeeds Act (2015)

The **Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA)**, signed by President Obama in 2015, replaced No Child Left Behind and represented a shift toward state flexibility while maintaining federal protections for vulnerable student populations. ESSA explicitly requires states to include students with disabilities, English Language Learners, and other marginalized groups in accountability systems — ensuring these students cannot be hidden from school performance data.

::::{note}
**Why This History Matters for You**

You are entering the classroom long after these battles were fought, but the battles are not over. Schools still exclude, resist, and underserve students with disabilities and students from marginalized communities. Understanding this history means understanding that inclusion is not a favor educators do for some students — it is a legal right that took decades of sacrifice to secure. Your job is to honor it.
::::

## The Medical Model vs. the Social Model of Disability

How you *think* about disability shapes everything about how you teach. Before you step into a classroom, you need to examine your assumptions — because most of us were raised in a culture that understands disability in a way that is fundamentally at odds with inclusive education.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-medical-vs-social-model.png
:label: fig-ch01-medical-vs-social-model
:alt: Infographic comparing the medical model and social model of disability
:width: 80%
:align: center

The medical model locates disability *in the person*. The social model locates disability *in the environment*. This difference has profound consequences for how teachers design their classrooms.
:::

### The Medical Model: Fixing the Person

The **medical model of disability** views disability as a problem located in an individual's body or brain — a deviation from the norm that needs to be diagnosed, treated, and if possible, cured or managed. Under this model, the goal of education is to bring the student as close to "normal" functioning as possible.

This model is not entirely wrong — there are real biological realities associated with many disabilities, and medical treatment genuinely improves quality of life for many people. But in educational settings, the medical model leads to deeply problematic outcomes:

- Students are defined by their deficits: "Johnny *can't* read," "Maria *has* autism," "Darius *suffers from* ADHD."
- The burden of change falls on the student: *they* must be fixed, normalized, or removed from the general setting if they cannot keep up.
- Teachers become gatekeepers: their job is to identify which students belong in the general education classroom and which students need to be sent elsewhere.
- Success is measured against a single "normal" standard — and students who don't meet it are labeled failures.

Many of your own schooling experiences were shaped by the medical model. When a classmate was pulled out of your third-grade class to go to "the resource room," that was the medical model in action — the system had identified something "wrong" with them that needed to be addressed elsewhere.

### The Social Model: Fixing the Environment

The **social model of disability**, developed by disability rights scholars and activists in the 1970s and 1980s, proposes a fundamental reframe: **disability is not located in the person. It is located in the mismatch between a person's characteristics and an environment that was not designed to include them.**

A wheelchair user is not disabled by their wheelchair. They are disabled by stairs. Remove the stairs and add ramps, and the disability — in the environmental sense — largely disappears.

A child with dyslexia is not disabled by their brain. They are disabled by a reading instruction system built entirely around phonetic decoding without multisensory supports. Change the instruction and provide text-to-speech technology, and their ability to access content transforms dramatically.

A child who is an English Language Learner is not disabled by their home language. They are disabled by a school system that treats their bilingualism as a deficit rather than an asset.

Under the social model, your job as a teacher is not to fix children. It is to fix environments. It is to design learning spaces, materials, and instruction that remove barriers and build access *for everyone*.

::::{tip}
**Practical Implication:** Before you ever refer a struggling student for a disability evaluation, ask yourself: *Have I changed the environment? Have I changed my instruction? Have I removed the barrier?* If the answer is no, start there.
::::

## Who Benefits from Inclusion?

One of the most persistent myths about inclusive education is that it benefits students with disabilities at the expense of students without disabilities — that including students who need extra support somehow slows down or dilutes the educational experience for everyone else.

The research does not support this. In study after study, across decades and countries, the evidence is clear: **inclusive education benefits all students.**

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-who-benefits-inclusion.png
:label: fig-ch01-who-benefits-inclusion
:alt: Infographic showing all stakeholders who benefit from inclusive K-5 classrooms
:width: 80%
:align: center

Inclusion creates a rising tide. The supports, flexible instruction, and welcoming culture that help students with disabilities thrive make *every* student's experience richer and more effective.
:::

### Benefits for Students with Disabilities

The research on outcomes for students with disabilities in inclusive settings compared to segregated settings is remarkably consistent:

- **Higher academic achievement.** Students with disabilities in inclusive classrooms make greater academic gains than their peers in segregated settings, across disability categories.
- **Better social outcomes.** Students with disabilities in inclusive settings develop stronger social skills, larger friendship networks, and greater self-advocacy skills.
- **Higher post-school outcomes.** Inclusive education is associated with higher rates of employment, post-secondary education enrollment, and independent living in adulthood.
- **Higher expectations.** General education teachers — and peers — hold higher academic expectations for students with disabilities than specialized settings often do.

A landmark 2001 study by researchers at the University of Minnesota found that students with significant disabilities who spent the majority of their time in general education classrooms outperformed similar students in segregated settings on measures of reading, math, social skills, and behavior — not by a small margin, but substantially.

### Benefits for Students Without Disabilities

Research on the academic outcomes of general education students in inclusive classrooms consistently shows *no negative effect* on achievement — and frequently shows positive effects. When teachers design instruction that is more flexible, more differentiated, and more engaging to reach a wider range of learners, *every* student benefits.

But the benefits go beyond academic outcomes:

- **Empathy and social awareness.** Children who learn and play alongside peers with disabilities develop greater empathy, social perspective-taking, and appreciation for human diversity — skills that research links to stronger leadership, teamwork, and life satisfaction.
- **Real-world preparation.** The actual world children will grow up to inhabit — their workplaces, neighborhoods, and communities — is diverse. Classrooms that reflect that diversity prepare children for real life in a way that homogeneous, segregated settings simply cannot.
- **Better self-regulation.** Inclusive classrooms often feature stronger behavioral and emotional supports (think: clear routines, co-regulation strategies, explicit social-emotional learning) that benefit all students, not just those with identified needs.

### Benefits for Teachers

Teachers who develop inclusive practice become better teachers — full stop. The skills required to reach a student with dyslexia, an ELL student, a child with ADHD, and a gifted student simultaneously are the same skills that make instruction excellent for everyone: clarity, flexibility, relationship, and relentless belief in every child's potential.

Teachers who retreat from inclusion often become narrower and more fragile as professionals. Teachers who lean into it grow.

## The Dimensions of Diversity You Will Encounter

Before you can design inclusive classrooms, you need to understand the full landscape of who "all students" actually includes. In a K–5 classroom, diversity is not a single dimension — it is a rich, overlapping web of characteristics, identities, and experiences.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-diversity-dimensions.png
:label: fig-ch01-diversity-dimensions
:alt: Infographic showing the many dimensions of diversity in a K-5 classroom surrounding a central "Whole Child" icon
:width: 80%
:align: center

Every child brings multiple dimensions of identity and experience to your classroom. Inclusive teaching requires awareness of all of them — not to reduce children to categories, but to see them fully.
:::

### Disability and Neurodiversity

Under IDEA, 13 disability categories qualify a student for special education services: specific learning disability, speech or language impairment, intellectual disability, emotional disturbance, autism spectrum disorder, developmental delay, traumatic brain injury, visual impairment, hearing impairment, deaf-blindness, orthopedic impairment, other health impairment, and multiple disabilities.

Approximately **7.5 million students** — about 15% of the K–12 population — receive special education services under IDEA. In addition, many students receive accommodations under Section 504 without qualifying for special education.

The neurodiversity movement, which began in the autism community and has since expanded, offers a reframe: rather than viewing ADHD, dyslexia, autism, and related profiles as disorders to be treated, neurodiversity perspectives see them as natural variations in human cognition — differences, not deficits. As an inclusive educator, you will hold both truths: you will understand the real challenges that come with many disabilities *and* you will see the genuine strengths, perspectives, and contributions that neurodivergent students bring.

### Language and English Language Learners

More than **5 million K–12 students** in the U.S. are classified as English Language Learners (ELLs) — students whose home language is not English and who are in the process of developing academic English proficiency. In many urban elementary schools, ELL students constitute 30%, 40%, or more of the student population.

ELL students are not students with disabilities. They are developing bilingualism — a cognitive and cultural asset of the highest order. And yet ELL students are frequently misidentified as having learning disabilities, tracked into low-level classes, and underserved by schools that mistake language acquisition for academic inability. We will spend significant time in Chapter 4 unpacking culturally responsive and linguistically supportive practice.

### Race, Ethnicity, and Racialized Experience

Race shapes every child's educational experience — through curriculum, through teacher expectations, through discipline practices, and through the images and stories children see (or do not see) reflected in school. Children of color, Black students in particular, are suspended and expelled at dramatically higher rates than white students for the same behaviors, a phenomenon researchers call the "discipline gap" or the "school-to-prison pipeline."

Building inclusive classrooms for students of color requires more than tolerance or "color-blindness" — it requires actively anti-racist practice, culturally affirming curriculum, and the dismantling of systems that systematically disadvantage children based on race.

### Socioeconomic Status

Poverty is one of the strongest predictors of academic outcomes in American education — not because poor children are less capable, but because poverty creates conditions (food insecurity, housing instability, trauma exposure, lack of access to healthcare and enrichment) that make learning harder. Approximately **18% of children** in the United States live in poverty.

Inclusive educators understand that Maslow's hierarchy is real: a child who is hungry, scared, or sleep-deprived cannot fully engage with academic learning. Before instruction comes safety, stability, and relationship.

### Gender Identity and Family Structure

K–5 students come from every family configuration — two-parent, single-parent, grandparent-led, foster, blended, same-sex parent, and more. Some students are navigating gender identity questions even in elementary school. Inclusive classrooms create environments where every family structure is normalized and every child's identity is respected.

### Giftedness

Students who are intellectually gifted — those whose cognitive abilities are significantly above grade-level expectations — are also learners with specific needs that the "one-size-fits-all" classroom fails to meet. Gifted students who are also members of marginalized groups (Black and Latino gifted students, gifted ELL students) are dramatically underidentified and underserved. Inclusive education includes them too.

## The Continuum of Supports

Inclusion does not mean that every student receives identical instruction in an identical setting with no differentiated support. It means that every student is educated in the setting that is *least restrictive* — and that robust, individualized supports are built into the system to make that possible.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-continuum-placement.png
:label: fig-ch01-continuum-placement
:alt: Infographic showing the continuum of special education placements from general education classroom to residential program
:width: 80%
:align: center

The Least Restrictive Environment principle means the general education classroom is always the presumed default. Movement to more restrictive settings requires data, documentation, and parent agreement — not assumptions.
:::

IDEA requires schools to make available a **continuum of placements** ranging from the general education classroom to residential programs. The presumed default is always the general education classroom — the least restrictive end of the spectrum. Movement toward more restrictive settings must be justified by data showing that education in the general setting cannot be achieved satisfactorily even with supplementary aids and services.

In practice, this means:

- **General education with in-class supports:** The student is in the general education classroom full time, with accommodations, modifications, and possibly a paraprofessional or co-teacher.
- **General education with pull-out services:** The student spends the majority of their time in the general education classroom but receives specific services (speech therapy, reading intervention, math support) in a separate setting for a portion of the day.
- **Resource room / self-contained classroom:** The student receives instruction in a separate classroom for some or most academic subjects.
- **Specialized day school:** A separate school serving students with similar disability profiles.
- **Residential program:** 24-hour care in a residential facility — the most restrictive setting, reserved for students with the most complex needs.

Research consistently shows that most students with disabilities — including students with significant intellectual disabilities and autism — can be meaningfully educated in general education settings with appropriate supports. The barriers to full inclusion are most often *attitudinal* and *systemic*, not rooted in the nature of any student's disability.

## Universal Design for Learning: The Architecture of Inclusion

If there is one framework you must internalize before you ever plan your first lesson, it is **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)**.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-udl-framework.png
:label: fig-ch01-udl-framework
:alt: Infographic of the Universal Design for Learning framework showing three principles and their application
:width: 80%
:align: center

UDL is not accommodation — it is architecture. It builds flexibility into the design of learning itself, so that fewer students need individualized compensatory supports after the fact.
:::

The term "universal design" originated in architecture. In the 1980s and 1990s, designers began building features into the built environment — ramps, automatic doors, curb cuts, closed captioning — that were intended to make spaces accessible to people with disabilities. What they discovered was that these features made the spaces better for *everyone*: parents with strollers used the curb cuts, delivery workers used the ramps, tired people used the automatic doors, and people learning English read the captions.

**Universal Design for Learning** applies the same principle to curriculum. Rather than designing instruction for the mythical "average" student and then retrofitting accommodations for everyone else, UDL asks: *How do we design learning from the start so that it is accessible and engaging for the widest possible range of learners?*

UDL is organized around three core principles:

### Multiple Means of Representation (The "What" of Learning)

Provide information in more than one format. Don't rely solely on text — use images, audio, video, physical models, and diagrams. Build vocabulary explicitly. Provide background knowledge. Offer glossaries and translations.

A child with dyslexia who struggles to decode text can access the same content through audio. An ELL student benefits from visual supports. A student with ADHD benefits from short, chunked information rather than long passages. And all students benefit from content presented in multiple modes — multimodal learning is simply more effective for human brains.

### Multiple Means of Action and Expression (The "How" of Learning)

Allow students to demonstrate their learning in multiple ways. Not every assessment needs to be a written test. Students might draw, build, speak, perform, create a video, or design a poster to show what they know.

This is not about lowering standards — it is about separating the *content knowledge* being assessed from the *format limitations* of a single type of assessment. A student with fine motor difficulties who cannot write legibly is not demonstrating less knowledge when they dictate their answer. They are demonstrating equal knowledge through a different format.

### Multiple Means of Engagement (The "Why" of Learning)

Provide multiple pathways to motivation and self-regulation. Some students are motivated by choice and autonomy; others need structure and predictability. Some thrive with challenge; others need to experience early success before they can take risks. Sustained engagement requires that students find personal relevance and value in what they are learning.

When teachers connect content to students' lives, cultures, interests, and identities — and provide choices in how students engage — they dramatically increase the percentage of learners who are genuinely invested.

::::{seealso}
The National Center on UDL maintains a free, research-grounded resource hub at **cast.org**. Their UDL Guidelines (version 3.0, released 2024) are the most current and comprehensive reference for applying UDL across grade levels and subject areas.
::::

## The Mindset of an Inclusive Educator

All the frameworks, laws, and strategies in this book are only as powerful as the beliefs of the teacher who applies them. Inclusive education is, at its root, a values question. And before you can build an inclusive classroom, you need to examine — honestly — what you believe about children.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-teacher-mindset.png
:label: fig-ch01-teacher-mindset
:alt: Infographic contrasting fixed mindset and growth mindset in inclusive educators
:width: 80%
:align: center

Your beliefs about students become self-fulfilling prophecies. Research consistently shows that teacher expectations are among the strongest predictors of student achievement — and that low expectations harm students just as surely as inadequate instruction.
:::

### The Research on Teacher Expectations

In 1968, psychologists Robert Rosenthal and Lenore Jacobson published *Pygmalion in the Classroom*, a landmark study in which they told elementary school teachers that specific students had been identified through a special test as "academic bloomers" who would show significant intellectual growth over the coming year. In reality, these students were selected at random.

At the end of the year, the "bloomers" — the students whose teachers had been led to expect growth — showed significantly greater IQ gains than their peers. Not because they were smarter. Because their teachers *believed* they were, and that belief changed how teachers interacted with them: more encouragement, more complex questions, more warmth, more patience.

The reverse is equally true, and far more common. When teachers hold low expectations for specific students — particularly students of color, students with disabilities, students from low-income backgrounds, and students who are ELLs — those expectations become self-fulfilling. Teachers give these students less challenging work, less wait time, less encouragement, and fewer opportunities to demonstrate what they can do. The students perform exactly as expected. And the teacher's bias is "confirmed."

### What Inclusive Educators Believe

Inclusive educators hold a specific set of beliefs that are non-negotiable. These are not naive optimism — they are empirically grounded commitments:

**Every child can learn.** Not in identical ways, not at identical paces, not toward identical endpoints — but every child is capable of meaningful growth. The question is never "Can this child learn?" The question is always "What conditions does *this specific child* need in order to learn?"

**All children belong in community with each other.** Segregation — by ability, disability, race, language, or any other characteristic — impoverishes every student it separates. Children learn best, develop best, and become the best versions of themselves in communities that reflect the full range of human diversity.

**Difference is not a problem to be solved.** The student who processes information differently, who moves through the classroom, who communicates in unexpected ways, who sees the world through a completely different lens — this student is not a problem. They are a perspective. They make your classroom more interesting, more creative, and more real.

**The system, not the child, is what needs fixing.** When a child is struggling, the inclusive educator's first instinct is not "What is wrong with this child?" It is "What is not working in my instruction, my environment, or my systems — and what can I change?"

::::{warning}
**Watch Your Language**

The words we use about students shape how we think about them — and how they think about themselves. Avoid deficit language: "low kids," "my special needs," "can't readers," "behavior problems." Use identity-first or person-first language consistently and thoughtfully (and when in doubt, ask students and families which they prefer). The language of limitation limits. The language of possibility opens doors.
::::

## The Belonging Pyramid: Meeting Every Child's Needs

Inclusion ultimately aims at something deeper than academic access. It aims at *belonging* — the profound human need to be seen, valued, and fully part of a community.

:::{figure} ../images/ch01-belonging-pyramid.png
:label: fig-ch01-belonging-pyramid
:alt: Infographic showing Maslow's hierarchy adapted for inclusive K-5 classrooms as a five-level pyramid
:width: 80%
:align: center

Before any child can fully engage with academic learning, their foundational needs — physical safety, emotional safety, and social connection — must be met. Inclusive classrooms are designed to address the whole pyramid.
:::

Adapted from Maslow's hierarchy, the **Belonging Pyramid for Inclusive Classrooms** helps you remember that academic learning sits at the top of a pyramid of needs — and that children cannot reliably reach the top without a stable foundation beneath them.

**Level 1 — Physical Safety and Access:** Every child can physically access the classroom, materials, and building. The environment is free from physical danger, sensory overwhelm, and architectural exclusion.

**Level 2 — Emotional Safety:** Every child is free from bullying, humiliation, and social exclusion. Every child knows that this is a place where they will not be mocked for the way they communicate, move, or learn.

**Level 3 — Social Connection:** Every child has at least one meaningful peer relationship. Social isolation is perhaps the most painful aspect of school for many students with disabilities and students from marginalized groups — and it is preventable with intentional community-building.

**Level 4 — Academic Engagement:** Every child has access to rigorous, appropriately challenging, and relevant academic content. This is where instruction, differentiation, and UDL live.

**Level 5 — Identity Affirmation and Agency:** Every child sees themselves reflected in the curriculum, feels their identity is honored rather than erased, and has genuine choice and voice in their own learning. This is the highest level of belonging — and the hardest to achieve, requiring the deepest level of inclusive commitment.

Your job is to build and protect the entire pyramid for every child in your room. Not just the academic layer.

## Glossary

```{glossary}
Inclusion
  The educational philosophy and practice of educating all students, including those with disabilities, in general education settings with appropriate supports — as full, valued members of the learning community.

Exclusion
  The historical practice of denying students with disabilities (and students from marginalized groups) access to public education entirely or in segregated settings.

Integration (Mainstreaming)
  The practice of placing students with disabilities in general education settings without substantially redesigning the system to support them — "getting in the door" without belonging.

Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
  The IDEA mandate that students with disabilities be educated in settings as close to the general education classroom as possible, with the supports necessary to succeed.

Free and Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)
  The IDEA guarantee that every student with a disability is entitled to a public education designed to meet their unique needs, at no cost to the family.

Individualized Education Program (IEP)
  A legally binding document developed by a team (including parents and teachers) that outlines a student's disability-related needs, annual goals, services, and placement.

Medical Model of Disability
  The perspective that disability is a deficit or pathology located within an individual, to be diagnosed, treated, and if possible corrected.

Social Model of Disability
  The perspective that disability results from the mismatch between an individual's characteristics and an environment that was not designed to include them — locating the problem in the environment, not the person.

Universal Design for Learning (UDL)
  A framework for designing flexible curricula that are accessible to the widest possible range of learners from the outset, rather than retrofitting accommodations after the fact.

Equity
  Providing each student with the specific resources and supports they need to access learning — as opposed to equality (giving every student the same thing).

Neurodiversity
  The natural variation in human cognitive profiles — including autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and related profiles — framed as differences rather than disorders.

English Language Learner (ELL)
  A student whose home language is not English and who is in the process of developing academic English proficiency.

Section 504
  A provision of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 that prohibits discrimination based on disability and requires schools to provide accommodations to students with disabilities who do not qualify for IDEA services.

Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)
  The federal law, first enacted as PL 94-142 in 1975 and most recently reauthorized in 2004, that guarantees students with disabilities the right to a free and appropriate public education in the least restrictive environment.

Pygmalion Effect
  The research-documented phenomenon in which teacher expectations about student ability become self-fulfilling — positively when expectations are high, negatively when they are low.

Belonging
  The deep human need to be seen, valued, and fully accepted as a member of a community — considered by inclusion researchers to be the ultimate goal of inclusive education, beyond mere access or participation.
```
